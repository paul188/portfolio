## Overview

As **Co-Lecturer** at the Universität Bonn, Institut für Informatik 4, I research and teach advanced cryptographic techniques applied to the specific challenges of sensitive medical data processing. The lecture covers **Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE)** and complementary privacy-preserving methods, grounding theoretical concepts directly in the real-world constraints of neuroimaging data infrastructure — the same domain I work in as a PhD candidate.

Students complete two practical labs implementing end-to-end cryptographic systems in Python, using Docker-based microservice architectures that mirror production healthcare data platforms.

---

## Application in Medicine / Neuroimaging

Medical data is uniquely sensitive: hospitals must share patient data for research while complying with DSGVO/GDPR, protecting patient privacy, and respecting strict institutional governance. FHE enables a three-party model that satisfies all of these simultaneously.

![FHE in neuroimaging: Researcher – DaRe (Data Trust) – Hospital sequence diagram](/images/projects/fhe-neuroimaging-diagram.png)

The diagram shows the protocol used in a real neuroimaging data trust context:

1. **Researcher** (Data User) sends a research query to **DaRe** (the Data Trust / Treuhaender)
2. **Hospital** (Data Provider) sends MRI scan data alongside patient demographics — age, gender, birthdate — encrypted with its own key <InlineMath math="k" /> as <InlineMath math="\text{Enc}_k(\text{PHI})" />. Raw PHI never leaves the hospital.
3. DaRe performs the **FHE computation** entirely on the ciphertext: <InlineMath math="f(\text{Enc}_k(\text{PHI})) = \text{Enc}_k(f(\text{PHI}))" />. Because DaRe holds no key, it has **no PHI access**. The encrypted result is sent back to the Hospital.
4. The Hospital **decrypts with key <InlineMath math="k" />**, reviews the result, and — after institutional approval — releases the plaintext result to the Researcher.

This architecture maps directly onto the lab exercises: the Treuhaender, DataProvider, and SPE components each implement one role in this flow.

## What is Fully Homomorphic Encryption?

- Compute *directly on ciphertext* — no decryption needed
- Result, once decrypted, equals plaintext computation
- Works for **any** function <InlineMath math="f" />
- The compute server **never sees the data**
- Key stays with the data owner at all times

The defining property:

<BlockMath math="\text{Dec}\!\left(f\!\left(\text{Enc}(x)\right)\right) = f(x)" />

## Lab 1 — FHE Correlation Analysis

### About the Project

This lab is developed in the **Cyber Security Lab Medical Data Privacy** to demonstrate privacy-preserving computation on sensitive medical data. The prototype uses a secure architecture inspired by real-world healthcare data sharing to compute the correlation between age and brain volume.

The focus is on a scenario where a data requester wants statistical insight, while sensitive patient-level information remains protected.

### Background and Motivation

Medical data is highly sensitive. In healthcare and research settings, institutions (e.g., hospitals) must balance enabling useful research, protecting patient privacy, and complying with legal requirements such as **DSGVO / GDPR**.

Traditional data analysis often requires sharing raw data, which increases privacy and compliance risks. This lab explores the secure alternative of using homomorphic encryption to compute useful statistics without exposing the raw data.

### Project Goal

The main implementation goals are:

- Calculating the correlation between **age** and **brain volume** while using homomorphic encryption
- Implementing a real-life-inspired architecture

For the lab, fake data is used for sensitive information and brain volumes to demonstrate functionality.

### Why FHE? Security over Efficiency

The focus of this project is not on optimising computational efficiency, but on **keeping data secure and patient information private**. FHE and similar privacy-preserving methods introduce computational overhead. In this project, that overhead is treated as an acceptable trade-off for stronger privacy.

### The Pearson Correlation Formula

The Pearson correlation coefficient between <InlineMath math="X" /> (age) and <InlineMath math="Y" /> (brain volume) over <InlineMath math="n" /> observations is:

<BlockMath math="r = \frac{n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i - \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \cdot \sum_{i=1}^n y_i}{\sqrt{\!\left(n\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 - \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\Bigr)^{\!2}\right) \cdot \left(n\sum_{i=1}^n y_i^2 - \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^n y_i\Bigr)^{\!2}\right)}}" />

The numerator and the squared terms are **sums of products** — operations FHE supports natively via ring addition and multiplication.

#### Statistical Confidence

Significance of <InlineMath math="r" /> is tested via the <InlineMath math="t" />-statistic under <InlineMath math="H_0: \rho = 0" />:

<BlockMath math="t = r \sqrt{\frac{n-2}{1-r^2}}, \quad t \sim t_{n-2}" />

For confidence intervals, Fisher's <InlineMath math="z" />-transformation stabilises the variance:

<BlockMath math="z = \tanh^{-1}(r) = \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{1+r}{1-r}\right), \quad z \;\dot{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}\!\!\left(\tanh^{-1}(\rho),\; \tfrac{1}{n-3}\right)" />

A 95% CI for <InlineMath math="\rho" /> is obtained by transforming <InlineMath math="\bigl[z \pm 1.96/\sqrt{n-3}\bigr]" /> back via <InlineMath math="\tanh" />.

#### Why Division and Inversion Are Hard in FHE

FHE schemes operate over **polynomial rings** — natively supporting addition and multiplication. Division and square roots have no closed polynomial form and must be approximated:

- **Division** <InlineMath math="(1/x)" />: approximated by Newton–Raphson — <InlineMath math="x_{k+1} = x_k(2 - ax_k)" /> — each iteration doubling the multiplicative circuit depth.
- **Square root** <InlineMath math="(\sqrt{x})" />: requires a polynomial approximation (Goldschmidt or Chebyshev series), adding further multiplicative levels.
- Each multiplicative level consumes noise budget; without bootstrapping, circuits of depth > 10–20 become infeasible.

**This lab computes the full correlation entirely under FHE.** Three strategies make this feasible:

**Strategy 1 — Newton–Raphson for the reciprocal:**

<BlockMath math="x_{k+1} = x_k\,(2 - a\,x_k) \;\xrightarrow{\;k\to\infty\;}\; 1/a" />

Quadratic convergence — 3–5 iterations suffice for correlation precision. Only multiplications and additions.

**Strategy 2 — Newton–Raphson for the inverse square root:**

<BlockMath math="y_{k+1} = \frac{y_k}{2}\!\left(3 - D\,y_k^2\right), \quad D = D_1 D_2 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; y \to \frac{1}{\sqrt{D}}" />

Then <InlineMath math="r = N \cdot y_\infty" /> — a single ciphertext multiplication.

**Strategy 3 — Chebyshev polynomial approximation** on a bounded interval <InlineMath math="[a,b]" /> (known from domain bounds on age and brain volume):

<BlockMath math="g(x) \approx \sum_{k=0}^{d} c_k\,T_k(x), \quad T_{k+1}(x) = 2x\,T_k(x) - T_{k-1}(x)" />

The three-term recurrence uses only multiplications and additions and is fully compatible with FHE. The **CKKS scheme** (via OpenFHE) handles rescaling after each multiplication automatically; bootstrapping refreshes the noise budget for deeper circuits.

### Data Flow

1. DataUser sends a research request to the Treuhaender
2. Treuhaender retrieves encrypted patient data from the DataProvider
3. Encrypted data is forwarded to the SPE
4. SPE computes <InlineMath math="N,\, D_1,\, D_2" /> homomorphically and returns the encrypted partial results
5. Treuhaender requests decryption from DataProvider
6. DataProvider decrypts; final <InlineMath math="r = N / \sqrt{D_1 D_2}" /> is returned to DataUser

The trust boundary is enforced by the **TrustClasses** module: any access outside a component's role raises a `TrustException`.

## Lab 2 — Private Set Intersection

### Goal of the Project

The main goal is to develop a **secure way for hospitals to determine which of their patients were also treated by other hospitals** — without revealing any additional patient information. The system satisfies these constraints:

- If outsiders gain access to the prepared patient records, they cannot derive any information about individual patients
- Each hospital learns **only** which of its own patients were treated by the other hospitals — no additional patient details, and no information about the other hospitals' patients
- The system is **efficient and scalable**
- Computation and data storage can be **outsourced to a Cloud Provider** without revealing patient data to that provider
- The system requires **no multi-party computation or shared secrets** between hospitals

### Why Not FHE for Set Intersection?

FHE is the right tool when you need to compute **non-trivial functions** (sums, products, correlations) on encrypted data. But PSI only asks a simpler question: *"are these two values the same?"* — a pure equality comparison.

For equality testing, FHE is severe overkill. Computing an encrypted equality check in FHE requires evaluating a polynomial approximation of the indicator function, which:

- Introduces high **multiplicative depth** and corresponding noise growth
- Inflates computation time by **10³–10⁶×** compared to a plaintext check
- Requires careful noise-budget management and potentially bootstrapping

**Hashing is far more efficient.** Argon2 is used — chosen for its **memory-hardness** property, which resists brute-force and GPU attacks on the hashed patient identifiers. Two records represent the same patient if and only if their hashes are identical. No raw PII reaches the server, and the intersection is computed in near-linear time. The right privacy tool must be chosen based on the *type of computation* required, not defaulting to the most powerful (and most expensive) technique.
