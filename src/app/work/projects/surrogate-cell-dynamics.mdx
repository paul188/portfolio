## Overview

Simulating how cells communicate through cytokines requires solving a reaction–diffusion PDE on complex 3D geometries — a computation that takes seconds to minutes per sample using finite-element methods. This project trains a **neural surrogate model** that learns the mapping from a spatial cell configuration to the steady-state cytokine concentration on each cell's surface, reducing inference to milliseconds. The application domain is the tumour microenvironment (InterpretTME), where the signalling dynamics between immune and tumour cells determine treatment response.

## 1. Governing PDEs

Cytokine transport in the extracellular space is governed by a **quasi-steady-state reaction–diffusion equation**:

<BlockMath math="D \,\Delta c(x) - \eta\, c(x) = 0 \qquad x \in \Omega" />

where <InlineMath math="c(x)" /> is the cytokine concentration, <InlineMath math="D" /> the diffusion coefficient, <InlineMath math="\eta" /> the first-order decay rate, and <InlineMath math="\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^3" /> the extracellular domain. The quasi-steady-state assumption is valid because cytokine diffusion equilibrates much faster than cell-level dynamics.

### Boundary Conditions

Cells act either as sources or sinks, encoded through Neumann boundary conditions on each cell surface.

**Secreting cells** <InlineMath math="\Gamma_{i,\text{sec}}" /> emit cytokine at a prescribed effective flux:

<BlockMath math="-D\,\frac{\partial c}{\partial n} = -q_{\text{eff}} \qquad \text{on } \Gamma_{i,\text{sec}}" />

**Absorbing cells** <InlineMath math="\Gamma_{i,\text{abs}}" /> take up cytokine via receptor-mediated endocytosis, modelled by a nonlinear Michaelis-Menten uptake function:

<BlockMath math="-D\,\frac{\partial c}{\partial n} = \psi(c,\, R) \qquad \text{on } \Gamma_{i,\text{abs}}" />

<BlockMath math="\psi(c,\, R) \;=\; \frac{k_{\text{endo}}\cdot R \cdot c}{\dfrac{k_{\text{off}}}{k_{\text{on}}} + c}" />

Here <InlineMath math="R" /> is the receptor count per cell, <InlineMath math="k_{\text{endo}}" /> the endocytosis rate constant, and <InlineMath math="k_{\text{off}}/k_{\text{on}}" /> the effective dissociation constant. At low concentrations the uptake is approximately linear in <InlineMath math="c" />; at high concentrations receptors saturate and uptake plateaus — this nonlinearity makes the problem particularly hard to approximate analytically.

The **outer boundary** of the domain imposes zero normal flux:

<BlockMath math="\frac{\partial c}{\partial n} = 0 \qquad \text{on } \partial\Omega" />

## 2. FEM Data Generation

Ground-truth surface concentrations are computed by solving the PDE above on 3D meshes using the **finite-element method (FEM)**. Each training sample is generated by:

1. Randomly placing **N = 50 spherical cells** in a cubic extracellular domain
2. Assigning each cell a type (secreting or absorbing) and a receptor count R drawn from a realistic biological range
3. Assembling the FEM system on a conforming tetrahedral mesh that resolves cell surfaces
4. Solving for the concentration field c(x) and averaging it over each cell's surface to obtain the target label

Each sample is stored as a tensor of shape **(6 × N)**:

**Data augmentation** exploits the physical symmetry of the problem: the concentration field is invariant under axis reflections and rotations by multiples of <InlineMath math="\pi/2" />. Applying these transformations multiplies the effective dataset size without any additional FEM solves. The trained model achieves a final loss of **~1 × 10⁻³** on held-out configurations.

## 3. Model Selection

The input to the model is a **point cloud** — an unordered set of N cell positions with associated per-point features (type, receptor count). The output is a scalar concentration per point. Three families of approaches were considered.

    <th style={{padding:'6px 10px',textAlign:'left'}}>Reason rejected</th>

    <tr style={{borderBottom:'1px solid var(--neutral-border-weak)'}}>

    </tr>
    <tr style={{borderBottom:'1px solid var(--neutral-border-weak)'}}>
      <td style={{padding:'6px 10px',color:'var(--neutral-on-background-weak)'}}>Voxel-based 3D CNNs</td>
      <td style={{padding:'6px 10px',color:'var(--neutral-on-background-weak)'}}>Voxelising 50 point-like cells in R³ yields an extremely sparse occupancy grid — most voxels are empty and convolutions waste compute. Fine enough resolution to resolve individual cell surfaces requires impractically large volumes, and the architecture does not generalise to a different number of cells without re-voxelisation.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td style={{padding:'6px 10px',color:'var(--neutral-on-background-weak)'}}>PointNet (selected)</td>
      <td style={{padding:'6px 10px',color:'var(--neutral-on-background-weak)'}}>Natively operates on unordered point sets. Permutation invariant by construction, matching the biological reality that cell indices are arbitrary. Scales to varying N without architectural changes.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

The problem is fundamentally **discrete** — a finite set of cells, each characterised by a handful of scalar features — and calls for a model whose inductive bias matches that structure directly.

## 4. The PointNet Architecture

<ClickableImage src="/images/projects/surrogate-architecture.png" alt="PointNet surrogate model architecture — encoder with attention and mean pooling, decoder MLP, skip connections" />

The architecture is a modified PointNet with four key design choices driven by the physics of the problem.

### Rotation-Invariant Input Encoding

Rather than feeding raw cell coordinates, an **autoencoder first maps the <InlineMath math="\frac{n(n-1)}{2}" /> pairwise inter-cell distances** into a latent space of dimension <InlineMath math="3N - 6" />. This eliminates the 3 translational and 3 rotational degrees of freedom of the cell configuration, producing a representation that is invariant under rigid motions of the entire system — matching the physical symmetry of the PDE without requiring explicit SO(3)-equivariant layers.

### Distance-Based Attention

Influence between cells is weighted by:

<BlockMath math="\text{softmax}\!\left(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2\right)" />

This mirrors the **inverse-square spatial decay** of concentration fields governed by the Laplacian (the Green's function of the Poisson equation decays as <InlineMath math="1/r" /> in 3D, and pairwise squared distances encode the same ordering). Cells further away receive exponentially smaller attention weights, embedding physical intuition directly into the architecture.

### Mean Pooling over Max Pooling

The global pooling step aggregates per-point features into a single latent vector. Standard PointNet uses max pooling to extract the most salient feature. Here **mean pooling** is used instead: the target quantity is the *average* surface concentration, which depends on contributions from all cells, not just the dominant one. Mean pooling preserves this global distributional information.

### Skip Connections and Decoder

Skip connections bridge the encoder bottleneck to the decoder input, allowing local per-cell features to bypass the global aggregation step. The decoder expands the concatenated global and local features through an MLP:

<BlockMath math="2048 \;\to\; 1024 \;\to\; 512 \;\to\; 256 \;\to\; 128 \;\to\; 64 \;\to\; 1" />

producing a single scalar per cell — the predicted mean cytokine concentration on that cell's surface. Permutation invariance is preserved throughout: the same MLP weights are applied independently to each point.
